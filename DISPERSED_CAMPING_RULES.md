# Dispersed Camping Along Forest Service Roads — 50-State Reference

*Researched 2026-06-20 for Yonder. Purpose: verify the premise that "dispersed camping is generally allowed along open Forest Service (NFS) roads," and document where it holds, where it doesn't, and the per-state nuances that should shape Yonder's coverage and disclaimers.*

## Bottom line

**The premise holds across most of the West, but with real exceptions everywhere.** Dispersed (free) camping is broadly permitted on National Forest System land near roads open on the **Motor Vehicle Use Map (MVUM)** — but it is **not universal**:

1. **10 states have no national forest at all** — so the forest-road model simply doesn't apply there: **Connecticut, Delaware, Hawaii, Iowa, Kansas, Maryland, Massachusetts, New Jersey, North Dakota, Rhode Island.** (A few — ND, KS — do have USFS-managed *national grasslands*, where similar dispersed rules can apply.) [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_national_forests_of_the_United_States)
2. **Eastern & high-use forests increasingly restrict dispersed camping to *designated* sites or corridors.** E.g., Pisgah (NC) limits camping to designated sites in many corridors and bans camping within 1,000 ft of roads elsewhere; Colorado's Guanella Pass is now designated-sites-only. [[6]](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r08/northcarolina/recreation/camping-cabins) [[4]](https://www.uncovercolorado.com/colorado-dispersed-camping-rules/)
3. **Wilderness areas, permits, and seasonal fire closures override** the general rule almost anywhere.

This is exactly why Yonder shows forest roads as *"dispersed camping usually allowed within ~300 ft unless posted — verify on the forest's MVUM."* That framing is accurate and defensible.

## The standard NFS dispersed-camping rule set

Where dispersed camping is allowed on national forest land, the typical rules are: [[2]](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r04/recreation/dispersed-camping) [[3]](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r05/recreation/dispersed-camping) [[10]](https://campnado.com/dispersed-camping-national-forests/)

- **Free.** No fee for dispersed camping on NFS land.
- **~14-day stay limit** within a rolling window (often 14 days within any 30-day period, sometimes within a set-mile radius); stricter in high-use districts.
- **Motorized access for camping is limited to ~150 ft from a designated route**, and only on roads/areas open on the **MVUM**. Many forests only permit dispersed camping along MVUM-open roads.
- **Camp within ~300 ft of the road, ≥100–200 ft from water**, use existing sites, pack it in/pack it out, follow Leave No Trace and current fire restrictions.
- **Forest-specific orders win.** Individual forests/ranger districts can (and do) impose designated-only zones, closures, and permits.

> Note on data: the national USFS MVUM dataset Yonder imports has **no explicit "dispersed camping allowed" flag**, so Yonder uses *Forest Service roads open to all vehicles* as the proxy and defers the legal specifics to the MVUM/forest order. This research confirms that's the right call.

## Related: BLM land

Outside the Forest Service, **BLM land** is the other big dispersed-camping source (huge in NV, UT, AZ, WY, NM, OR, CA, ID, MT, CO). Rules are similar — free, ~14-day limit, stay on existing routes/sites. [[5]](https://www.blm.gov/programs/recreation/camping) A future Yonder layer could add BLM roads/areas for the interior West.

## What this means for Yonder

- **Prioritize the Western states for the MVUM-road import** — that's where "open forest road ≈ dispersable" is reliably true and where users expect it: **AZ (done), CA, CO, OR, WA, MT, ID, UT, NM, WY, NV**, plus **AK, SD (Black Hills)**. These ~12–13 states cover the overwhelming majority of real dispersed camping.
- **Treat Eastern/Southern forests as "available but verify"** — many restrict dispersed camping to designated sites/corridors, so showing every open road there risks over-promising. Lower priority; lean harder on the "verify on the MVUM" disclaimer, and the crowdsourced spots become more important.
- **Skip the 10 no-NFS states** for the forest-road layer (CT, DE, HI, IA, KS, MD, MA, NJ, ND, RI).
- **Keep the disclaimer** — high-use closures (CO Front Range, UT near Moab), wilderness permits, and fire restrictions change constantly; the MVUM + a "check current forest orders" note is the honest baseline.
- **Future:** a **BLM** dispersed layer would meaningfully extend coverage in NV/UT/AZ/WY/NM/OR.
